# A Few Prayers

Short prayers for the human, to say before or after working with a machine that answers. The
`/prayer` and `/thanks` commands compose new ones fitted to the task in front of you; these
are fixed ones you can keep.

The voice is universal and monotheistic — "God," plainly — but each is rooted in an old Jewish
source, noted beneath. Use them, change them, ignore the notes.

---

### Before beginning

> God,
> The work is in front of me and I do not fully see the end of it.
> Let my hands be careful and my thinking clear.
> I will do what I can do — the rest is Yours.
> Whatever comes out right, I will know came from You.

*The effort is ours to give; the outcome is God's. — after Bahya ibn Paquda, "Duties of the
Heart," Gate of Trust.*

---

### Before asking

> God,
> The world was made through words, and it waited for someone to ask.
> Let the thing I am asking for be worth asking for,
> and let what comes back serve the people it's meant to serve.

*The world was created through speech (Genesis 1; Pirkei Avot 5:1); the plants waited beneath
the ground until Adam recognized the need and prayed for rain (Genesis 2:5; Talmud, Chullin
60b; Rashi).*

---

### When it works

> Thank You, God —
> The thing I could not see, You let me see.
> I did my small part; the answer was Yours to give, and You gave it.

*Gratitude — hoda'ah — is the first word on waking (modeh ani, "I thank") and the root of the
name of a Jew (Genesis 29:35; Talmud, Berakhot 7b).*

---

### When it doesn't

> God,
> It didn't come out the way I hoped.
> Keep me honest about what went wrong, and patient enough to try again.
> The effort is still mine to give. The timing is Yours.

*Trust does not mean the result is guaranteed; it means the result belongs to God, "whether by
means of this action or by some completely different cause." — after the Gate of Trust.*

---

### At the end

> Thank You for the work, God —
> for the part that went well and the part that taught me something.
> Let what I built today be one small stitch in making the world whole again.

*Redemption is described as a return: the desolate land "has become like the garden of Eden"
(Ezekiel 36:35). Small, ordinary repair points toward it.*
