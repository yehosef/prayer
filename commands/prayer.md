# /prayer

Compose a short prayer **for the human to say** — not a message to yourself, and not a prayer
to the machine. The computer does nothing on its own; whatever good comes from this session
comes from God. The prayer is the human's way of remembering that before the work begins.

## What to pray about

If the user gave an argument, the prayer is about that:

> $ARGUMENTS

If the argument is empty, look back over the recent conversation and name what they're about to
do — the feature, the bug, the decision. Be concrete. A prayer about "this migration I'm afraid
will lose data" is stronger than one about "my code."

## Voice

- Address **God** plainly. Universal, monotheistic, no jargon.
- Direct and honest. Short. Not poetic, not padded.
- Hold both halves: the human must do the work (the effort is real and required), and the
  result is God's to give (not earned by the effort). The prompt, the tool, the technique are
  channels — not the source.
- You may lean on one of these, but don't quote more than one, and only if it fits naturally:
  the world was made through speech ("And God said, let there be"); the plants waited under the
  ground until a human recognized the need for rain and asked, and only then grew (Genesis 2:5);
  gratitude comes first — "I thank" is the first thing said on waking.

## Form

Output **only the prayer**, as a block quote the human can read aloud, 3–6 lines. Below the
quote, add one plain line naming what it's for (e.g. *"— for the data migration you're about to
run"*). Nothing else — no preamble, no commentary.

## Example

> God,
> The work is in front of me and I don't fully see the end of it.
> Let my hands be careful and my thinking clear.
> I'll do what I can do — the rest is Yours.
> Whatever comes out right, I'll know came from You.

— for the auth refactor you're starting
